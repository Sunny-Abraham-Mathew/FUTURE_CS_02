# FUTURE_CS_02
# 📧 Phishing Email Investigation Report

## Overview

This repository contains an analysis of five phishing email samples collected for Task 2 at Future Interns Internship. Each email was manually reviewed to identify social engineering techniques, impersonation attempts, suspicious sender information, and indicators of compromise.

The objective was to understand how phishing campaigns target victims through deception, urgency, trust abuse, and credential harvesting tactics.

---

## Project Goals

* Examine real-world phishing email examples
* Identify common social engineering techniques
* Analyze sender information and email content
* Categorize threats based on risk level
* Document indicators that help users recognize phishing attempts
* Promote security awareness through practical examples

---

## Analysis Approach

Each email was reviewed using the following process:

1. Inspect sender information
2. Review subject line and message content
3. Identify impersonation attempts
4. Detect urgency, fear, or reward-based tactics
5. Examine requests for credentials, money, or personal information
6. Assess overall risk level
7. Document findings and recommendations

---

## Threat Categories Observed

### Employment Scam Phishing

Examples offering remote jobs, internships, or easy income opportunities in exchange for personal information.

### Government Impersonation

Messages pretending to be government agencies requesting sensitive information.

### Disaster Relief Fraud

Emails abusing natural disaster assistance programs to steal credentials.

### Financial Institution Impersonation

Fake payment notifications and invoice scams designed to trigger panic.

### Attachment-Based Recruitment Scams

Job offers containing potentially malicious attachments.

## Common Indicators Identified

* Generic greetings
* Sender domain mismatch
* Requests for personal information
* Requests for credentials
* Urgency and pressure tactics
* Impersonation of trusted organizations
* Suspicious contact information
* Unverified attachments
* Financial incentives used as bait
* Fear-based messaging

## Key Takeaways

* Sender identity should never be trusted without verification.
* Legitimate organizations rarely request sensitive information through email.
* Domain mismatches remain one of the strongest phishing indicators.
* Urgent requests involving money, credentials, or personal information should always be verified independently.
* Security awareness remains one of the most effective defenses against phishing attacks.
